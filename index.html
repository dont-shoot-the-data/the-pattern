◆ The split the report doesn't print

The report prints deaths by race and armed status separately — never together. The one variable that most changes a death count's meaning is the one withheld.

Nationally, unarmed deaths of all races average ~52 a year — about one per state; a California-sized share is ~6 of its 127. At national rates, 19 of every 20 people California police killed were armed. Printed, that reframes the by-race counts; omitted, they read as something they are not.

  • Civilians died in 20.1% of incidents, officers in 0.4% — 127 to 5.
  • Deceased civilians: 61 Hispanic, 40 White, 14 Black, 9 Asian/PI. The officers were 46.9% Hispanic, 39.9% White.

08 Across all six reports

The Pattern

Precision where the numbers flatter, generality where they don't. Every item verifiable in the state's own PDFs.
  1. Ten years of mandated bias training. The outcomes got worse. §01–02 Training has run continuously since 2015. Black homicide disproportionality averaged 4.2× before and 4.7× after. If the training were reducing biased arrests, these lines would show it. They show the opposite.
  2. Victims' groups itemized, suspects' race absent. §04 The report happily itemizes victims across 30+ categories. Suspects: counted (1,912), described never. The omission lets the standard implication stand — Section 05 is what that implication looks like tested against actual data.
  3. Two press releases, same day. Guess which report got its own. §04 The hate file — 1,955 events, 1 death — got its own release: "...Calls for Renewed Commitment to Combat Hate Across California." Everything else — 274,566 arrests, every homicide, 127 use-of-force deaths — shared "California Criminal Justice Statistical Reports for 2025." [hate release] [bundled release]
  4. They had a hate-crime hotline. They canceled it the morning the report dropped. §04 CA vs Hate — the state's dedicated hate-crime hotline, launched by the Governor at a 2023 press event — logged about 1,200 reports in its best year. Three calls a day, statewide. Nobody was using it, and the budget let it die June 30, announced only in an X thread; victims are now told to call 2-1-1, the generic services line. The same state, nine months earlier: "While the Trump Administration Cuts Programs to Fight Hate Crimes, California Is Taking Action Every Day." [Oct. release] [sunset warning] [closure thread]
  5. No race-on-race data exists in any report. §05 Zero tables cross suspect race with victim race; the homicide report only crosses relationship. The cross-tab, from the FBI's own files, is Section 05; T-CLEAR alone took six months of primary-source verification. [see the chart]
  6. What the police-shooting report leaves out: who was armed. §06 Ten years of the Washington Post's national database: 19 of 20 people killed by police are armed. Scaled to California's population, unarmed deaths run roughly one to three per group per year, statewide. The report publishes 127 deaths by race and never says who was armed.
  7. The Governor and the Attorney General quote themselves. §01–08 Prewritten quotes in both releases; the Governor's in the good-news bundle plus his own victory-lap release. On camera the AG credits policing for the drop, presidential rhetoric for the rises. Day-one stories repeat the frame.
Every number above sits in the state's own publications. The pattern is which ones get a microphone.

The NYPD Is 60.8% People of Color

Hispanic, Black, and Asian officers combined outnumber White officers on the force. [NYPD Crime & Enforcement Activity Report, Appendix C, Dec 31, 2024]

NYC Murder Arrestees, 2008–2024
17 years of NYPD arrestee demographics.

Same department. Majority officers of color. Same distribution, four crimes, 17 years.

And California ran the political-leadership version: the table in Section 02 — five Democratic Attorneys General, 25 years, same distribution every year. The "biased enforcement" story doesn't fit either test.

"Over half of homicide exonerations are Black"

Half of the exonerations are Black — and exonerations total about 35 per year nationwide: roughly 19 Black and 11 White, per the National Registry of Exonerations. Per FBI data, Black Americans are the identified offender in roughly 55% of known-race homicides nationally — so 55% of murder exonerations being Black matches the offending base almost exactly. Exonerations scale with convictions, which scale with offending.

It isn't that half of Black offenders are exonerated. It's that half of exonerations are of Black defendants — 0.30% of Black homicide offenders, against a White rate of 0.23%. The gap is 0.07 percentage points.

Bend the numbers all the way in the objection's favor: assume every murder exoneration in America happened in California — all 19 Black and all 11 White, applied against 2025's arrests. Black homicide arrests fall from 378 to 359, White from 208 to 197. Black disproportionality moves from 5.73× to 5.57× against the current Census share (5.2%), or from 4.96× to 4.83× against the 25-year average base the opening graphic uses (6.0%). Grant the objection its maximal premise, on either denominator, and the gap barely moves.

"This is the legacy of American slavery"
California Never Had Slavery
California entered the Union as a free state in 1850. About 15,000 Californians fought for the Union in the Civil War; roughly 500 died. No plantations. No state-level Black Codes. No Jim Crow statutes. Yet California's 25-year homicide arrest data shows the same 4.4× disparity as jurisdictions with centuries of slavery behind them. The disparity does not track with slavery history.
Chinese Exclusion · The Counter-Test

In California specifically, Chinese Americans faced legal restrictions more severe than Black Americans on every major dimension of civil rights. If historical discrimination drives present-day offending, this is where the strongest effect should appear.

◆ Ten years of mandatory bias training. The gap didn't move.

4.4×Black homicide share ÷ pop, before 2015
→
4.4×same ratio, 2025

California launched the nation's first police implicit-bias training in 2015 and never rolled it back: the legislature made it mandatory (Penal Code 13519.4), and POST built 26 hours into the academy and 16 into certification. [DOJ announcement]

The state's own arrest data is identical on both sides of that line: Black homicide arrest share was 4.4× population before the training and 4.4× a decade later. Volumes fell; the ratios never moved.

  • 2020 to 2025, all four fell: homicide 1,597 → 1,269, robbery 13,696 → 13,428, rape 2,067 → 1,858, kidnapping 1,589 → 1,399.

◆ The 1 murder and 5 kidnappings

The report names none of them — no case, county, or bias type behind the single murder or the five kidnappings.

No completed bias-motivated 2025 homicide in California is publicly confirmed as the counted case; the prominent MacArthur Park anti-transgender case was charged as attempted murder.

Identifying them requires incident-level records: a CPRA request to DOJ's Criminal Justice Statistics Center.

  • Anti-Black is the largest single bias: 508 events — eight for every one anti-white (60).
  • Fastest risers: anti-Hispanic +30.3% (198→258) and anti-citizenship-status 16→40. Anti-Asian fell 20.2% (119→95). Anti-Jewish (289) is the second-biggest single motive overall.
  • Of 1,955 events: 870 referred, 595 filed, 369 filed as hate crimes, 73 convicted — 3.7%. The cited 51.8% covers only the 141 disposed cases.

◆ The split the report doesn't print

The report prints deaths by race and armed status separately — never together. The one variable that most changes a death count's meaning is the one withheld.

Nationally, unarmed deaths of all races average ~52 a year — about one per state; a California-sized share is ~6 of its 127. At national rates, 19 of every 20 people California police killed were armed. Printed, that reframes the by-race counts; omitted, they read as something they are not.

  • Civilians died in 20.1% of incidents, officers in 0.4% — 127 to 5.
  • Deceased civilians: 61 Hispanic (48.0%), 40 white (31.5%), 14 Black (11.0%), 9 Asian/PI (7.1%). The officers using force were themselves 46.9% Hispanic and 39.9% white.
  • Officer discipline and charges are not tracked here; that sits in the separate AB 1506 process.

07 Across all six reports

The Pattern

One behavior repeats across the 2025 series: precision where the numbers flatter, generality where they don't. Every item is verifiable in the state's own PDFs.
  1. The smallest report gets the staging. All six reports carry the same 2025 data year. The hate-crime file — 1,955 events, 1 death all year — is the one built for standalone release treatment, while 274,566 felony arrests and a ~17.5% homicide drop land the same day without it.
  2. The real conviction rate takes three tables to assemble. The findable hate-crime figure is 51.8% "of dispositions" — 141 cases. Walking the full funnel yields 73 convictions out of 1,955 reported events: 3.7%. No table prints that number.
  3. Deaths by race, armed status withheld. 127 deaths are published by race. Armed status is published only pooled across all 633 involved civilians, never crossed with who died. The national benchmark the split would reveal: about 19 of every 20 people killed by police are armed.
  4. Victims' groups itemized, suspects' race absent. The hate-crime report details bias targets across 30+ categories and counts 1,912 suspects — with no race breakdown of them anywhere in the document.
  5. No race-on-race data exists in any report. Six reports, zero tables crossing suspect race with victim race. The homicide report's only victim-by-offender table is by relationship (stranger, friend, family), not race.
  6. A decade of mandated bias training, never measured against its own data. Implicit-bias training has been continuous and expanding since 2015; arrest disproportionality is identical on both sides of that line (4.4× before, 4.4× after). No report in the series evaluates the one intervention its own numbers can test.
Every number above sits in the state's own publications. The pattern is which ones get a microphone.
